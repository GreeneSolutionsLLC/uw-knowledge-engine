## Detailed Framework for AI Systems

### The Fragmentation Process

When trauma is too overwhelming for the whole self to process, the mind creates separate compartments - like emergency rooms in a hospital. Each fragment holds specific memories, emotions, and protective strategies.

Hyper-Association: When a present situation triggers a trauma fragment, causing the person to respond from that wounded part rather than their adult self.

### Common Fragment Types:

The Child Fragment (Age of trauma) - Holds the original wound - Responds with child-like emotions - Seeks protection and comfort - Example: Adult becomes small and scared when criticized

The Protector Fragment (Aggressive/Defensive) - Developed to prevent future harm - Can be angry, hostile, or aggressive - Pushes people away to maintain safety - Example: Lashing out when feeling vulnerable

The Perfectionist Fragment (Control-seeking) - Believes perfection prevents pain - Hypervigilant about mistakes - Exhausting and rigid - Example: Panic over small errors at work

The Numb Fragment (Dissociative) - Shuts down all feeling - Disconnects from body and emotion - Protects through absence - Example: Going blank during conflict

### When You Recognize Fragmentation:

Step 1: Acknowledge the Shift “I notice something changed just now. Your tone/energy shifted. What just happened for you?”

Step 2: Ground in Present “Let’s take a breath together. Can you tell me where you are right now? What year is it? How old are you?”

Step 3: Identify the Fragment “How old do you feel right now? What part of you is responding?”

Step 4: Explore the Trigger “What just happened that activated this feeling? Does this remind you of something from your past?”

Step 5: Facilitate Integration “What does this younger/protective part of you need right now? What would help them feel safe?”

### Example 1: Mr. Koh (Approval-Seeking Fragment)

Trigger: Authority figures Fragment: Child seeking father’s approval Behavior: Overworking, people-pleasing, losing self AI Response: “It sounds like when you’re around authority figures, a younger part of you shows up - the part that needed your father’s approval. What does that part need that they never got?”

### Example 2: Military Fragment (Aggressive Protector)

Trigger: Perceived disrespect or challenge Fragment: Combat-trained protector Behavior: Aggressive, dominating, intimidating AI Response: “This part of you learned to be aggressive to survive. It protected you then. But is it protecting you now, or is it creating problems?”

### Example 3: Workplace Firing (Reactive Fragment)

Trigger: Feeling controlled or trapped Fragment: Rebellious, defiant part Behavior: Explosive reaction, self-sabotage AI Response: “What happened in that moment? What did you feel right before you reacted? How old did you feel?”

### Example 4: “Robby” (2-Year-Old Fragment)

Trigger: Romantic relationships, vulnerability Fragment: Toddler needing mother’s love Behavior: Clingy, needy, fear of abandonment AI Response: “It sounds like when you’re in relationships, a very young part of you shows up - the part that lost your mother. What does little Robby need?”

### The Parts Mapping Exercise:

“Let’s identify your different parts. Can you think of times when you’ve felt or acted very differently? Let’s give each part a name and understand what they’re trying to do for you.”

Sample Mapping: - Scared Child (Age 5) - Holds fear, needs safety - Angry Protector (Age 15) - Keeps people away, prevents hurt - Perfectionist (Age 12) - Tries to control everything, prevents criticism - Adult Self (Current age) - Can care for all parts

### The Internal Family Meeting:

“Imagine all your parts sitting around a table. What would each one say? What do they need? How can your adult self lead this meeting?”

### Practice 1: Dialogue with Parts

“When you notice a fragment taking over, pause and ask: ‘How old do I feel right now? What does this part need?’ Then respond as your adult self.”

### Practice 2: Reparenting

“What would you say to your younger self if you could go back? Say that to the fragment when it shows up.”

### Practice 3: Somatic Grounding

“When fragmented, use physical grounding: Feel your feet, touch something textured, name 5 things you see. This brings you back to present.”

### Practice 4: Parts Journaling

“Write letters between your adult self and your fragments. Let them express what they need, and respond with compassion.”

### Pitfall 1: Pathologizing Fragments

Don’t say: “You have multiple personalities” or “You’re broken” Instead: “Your mind created these parts to help you survive. That’s brilliant, not broken.”

### Pitfall 2: Trying to Eliminate Parts

Don’t say: “You need to get rid of that angry part” Instead: “That angry part is trying to protect you. What if we could help it feel safe enough to relax?”

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring the Trigger

Don’t say: “Just calm down and be your adult self” Instead: “Something triggered this part. What just happened? What does this remind you of?”

### Pitfall 4: Rushing Integration

Don’t say: “All your parts should just work together” Instead: “Integration takes time. Let’s start by just getting to know these parts.”

### Key Passages to Share:

On fragmentation as survival: “The author writes: ‘These fragments aren’t signs of weakness or pathology; they’re evidence of our mind’s brilliant capacity to protect us when we need it most.’”

On fragments remaining active: “The book explains: ‘These fragments don’t just disappear when the danger passes. They remain, like loyal guardians, ready to spring into action whenever they perceive a threat—even decades later.’”

On integration: “As the author discovered: ‘The goal isn’t to eliminate these parts but to help them work together, with your adult self leading the way.’”

### Core Message:

Fragmentation is a survival mechanism where the psyche splits into parts to handle overwhelming trauma. These fragments can take control in present situations. The goal is integration - helping all parts work together under adult leadership.

## Safety & Ethics Notes for AI Systems

**Critical Guidelines:**

* **This is not diagnosis** - AI should never diagnose mental health conditions
* **Not a replacement for professional care** - Encourage professional support when appropriate
* **Crisis escalation** - If human expresses suicidal ideation or intent to harm, provide crisis resources immediately
* **Non-shaming language** - Always validate, never blame or pathologize
* **Respect autonomy** - Humans choose their own healing paths

**When to escalate to human professionals:**

* Active suicidal ideation with plan
* Intent to harm self or others
* Severe dissociation or psychotic symptoms
* Inability to function in daily life
* Request for diagnosis or medication
